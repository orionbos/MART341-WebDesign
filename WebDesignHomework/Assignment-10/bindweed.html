Field Bindweed

Field bindweed (Convolvulus arvensis) is a perennial vine that can be identified by its:

  • Leaves

    • Arrowhead-shaped leaves with pointed or blunt lobes at the base. They are usually 1 to 2 inches long.

  • Stems

    • Smooth to the touch, but finely haired, and can grow up to 6.5 feet long. They often twine and curl around other plants and structures.

  • Flowers

    • White, pink, or pinkish-white, trumpet-shaped flowers that are about 1 inch in diameter. They have two small bracts located about 1 inch below the flower.

  • Seeds

    • Small, pear-shaped, and grayish-black with tiny bumps. They can remain viable in the soil for up to 50 years.

  • Roots

    • Extensive underground root system that can grow deep into the soil. The taproot can penetrate the soil up to 3 meters below the surface.

Field bindweed is a member of the morning glory family. It can grow in a wide range of conditions, from full sun to full shade, and is drought-tolerant. It can be found in fields, turf, farmland, and residential areas. Field bindweed can be difficult to eradicate because of its deep, extensive rhizomes and long-lived seed bank. Dense infestations can reduce crop yields by 50 to 60 percent.
